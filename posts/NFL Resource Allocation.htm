Steven Liao, as part of a Final Project for Brown‚Äôs CS1951a Data Science Course


In 2017, in a ‚Äògroundbreaking‚Äô, ‚Äòstunning‚Äô deal, the Cleveland Browns acquired the maligned Brock Osweiler from the Texans, and received a 2018 second round pick for their troubles.

From an asset perspective, the Browns traded $15 million in cap space (per Spotrac ) for a player and a second round draft pick. While trading liquid assets (cap space) for draft picks is nothing new in the NBA, this was one of the first instances of it happening in the NFL. The Browns, with their new, analytics-driven braintrust, received praise for their ingenuity.

Yet, was the Browns trade a good one? Considering the Browns eventually cut Osweiler, the Browns essentially traded 15 million dollars for a second round pick ‚Äì was this too high a price to pay?

What would be a fair price? One million? Five million? Is it possible to calculate a ‚Äòfair value‚Äô, in terms of cap space, for a draft pick?

Project Overview

Back in 2005, a Canadian blogger named Kyle MacDonald took a humble red paperclip, and over a series of fourteen swaps, ended up with a two-story house.

An NFL General Manager plays a barterer. The General Manager takes a boatload of cash (however much the salary cap is), and must make exchanges where hopefully, each exchange increases the overall value of the assets under the team‚Äôs control.

Indeed, value is the key word ‚Äì when acquiring a player, General Managers are also acquiring their contracts. Value is why we often see great players flipped for chump change ‚Äì for instance, in 2014, when the Patriots traded Pro Bowl guard Logan Mankins for a fourth round pick and Tim Wright, a backup tight end. The biggest return the Patriots received in this trade was unspoken: the freed salary space from dumping Mankins‚Äô big contract.

With this in mind, we can model the NFL has having three primary assets: cash (cap space), players, and draft picks. An NFL General Manager playing professional barterer is necessarily concerned with the following questions:

  1. How much is a given player worth in cap space?
  2. How much is a given draft pick worth in cap space?

The second question is of much more interest to me. Valuing draft picks feels like a much more tricky proposition than valuing players. Some draft picks make the team, others do not. Some draft picks pan out, others do not. Draft picks can‚Äôt contribute immediately ‚Äì so in trading for a draft pick, you‚Äôre trading present value (and / or cap space liquidity) for a future value.

The inherent difficulties in valuing draft picks can make it easy for biases to impact decision-making. Ample previous analytics work on the NFL draft suggests general managers tend to over-value top draft picks, with one explanation being that general managers are tempted to play the lottery and establish a reputation as ‚Äògeniuses‚Äô. Biases can also cause us to undervalue draft picks; how many times after a team trades away a draft pick, have you heard the justification, ‚Äúthe draft is nothing more than a crapshoot?‚Äù

So, in this project, I sought to establish an ‚Äòobjective‚Äô (by this I mean clearly and hopefully intelligently defined, not ‚Äòabsolutely correct‚Äô üòâ) measure of a draft pick‚Äôs worth in terms of salary cap space ‚Äì in other words, answer the question, ‚ÄúIs a second round pick worth $15 million?‚Äù

Methodology

The intuition behind this project is simple, breaking down into the following steps:

  1. We use historical AV data to determine how much a draft pick contributes, on average, over the first four years of the rookie contract.
  2. We devise a ‚Äòfair salary‚Äô metric which converts AV contribution to how much the player deserves to be paid. We can use this to compute a ‚Äòfair salary‚Äô for a given draft pick.
  3. We compare the draft pick‚Äôs fair salary with their actual salary (which is relatively fixed due to changes in the CBA in 2011). The difference is the surplus, which we use to value a draft pick.

This is a good segue into the assumptions our model makes:

  1. The value of a draft pick is equal to its absolute surplus, defined as (fair salary) ‚Äì (actual salary). For instance, if the first overall pick deserves a contract worth $40 million dollars over 4 years, but you're paying them $30 million, then the first overall pick's absolute surplus (value) is $10 million dollars (over 4 years).

    An argument could be made that valuation should be instead percentage return ([fair value ‚Äì actual value]/ actual value)- so in the previous example, the percentage return is 1/3. However, recall that only a small portion of a team's salary cap is spent on draft picks each year, and any money not spent on draft picks can be imagined as offering 0% return above fair value in free agency (see assumption 2 below).

    For instance, the Indianapolis Colts are currently spending roughly $16 million on drafted players on rookie contracts, or about 10.3% of their total spending (and not much more if we included undrafted rookie free agents on rookie contracts).

    To put it another way, I‚Äôd rather spend $20 million on rookie contracts for $30 million in value ($10 million surplus), than spend $10 million on rookie contracts for $18 million in value ($8 million surplus) ‚Äì because the unspent money in both cases will have $0 surplus!

  2. Any 'purchase' via free agency returns market value. In other words, there is no surplus on free agent contracts; an important implication of this is that no team has an edge in free agency. This is based on the notion that free agency resembles an open market, so we can assume that every contract is 'fair' (no maximum contract 'caps' like in the NBA). This, of course, is not true in practice necessarily (i.e. hometown discounts, occasional bargains / fleeces) but serves well in simplifying our model.

    Another important implication of this assumption is that a draft‚Äôs value lasts only as long as the drafted players are on rookie contracts, as afterwards, the players in those draft are no longer playing for a discount. So after five years, a good or bad draft class no longer has an effect on how good a team is. One recent example where this implication appears to hold weight is the modern-day Seahawks ‚Äì the Seahawks had legendary drafts from 2010-2012, and fielded great teams in 2013-2014. After the players began signing their second deals, the Seahawks have fallen off a bit (but are still quite good). This also means that in modern-era NFL, dynasties aren‚Äôt built through the draft, as the advantage of a strong draft lasts just five years at most.

Part One: Fair Salary Metric

As noted in the second step of the methodology, we created a ‚Äòfair salary‚Äô metric based on Approximate Value (AV) scores. AV is a metric developed by ‚ÄãPro Football Reference which serves as a proxy for the performance of a player over the course of a season. We use AV because it is objective, comparable across positions (which simplifies the analysis), and because it is rather well-known (it is a common proxy for player performance in other analytics projects). Read more here .

We used Python‚Äôs Beautiful Soup library to scrape AV data (from Pro Football Reference) and salary data (from Spotrac) from 1994 (beginning of salary cap era) to 2016, and stored it in a SQL database.

We used R to process this data, and calculated the fair AV value for each player as follows, starting with the following assumptions:

  • AV contribution is linear. I.e. a player whose AV is 8 in a given year is worth twice as much as a player whose AV is 4 in a given year.
  • A player with 0 AV is worth $0
  • A practice squad player‚Äôs pay is negligible

Using these three assumptions, and inputting the salary cap for every year from 1994 to 2016 (ranging from 34.6 million in 1994 to 155.27 million in 2016), we were able to calculate the fair salary for each player in that given year, using the following formula:

  • Let SC = total combined salary cap across all 32 teams (167 million times 32 for 2017)
  • Let N = total number of players who can be on an NFL roster (53 times 32 for 2017)
  • Let AVTotal = Summed AV of the top N NFL players in a given season

Then Fair Salary is p_av/AVTotal * SC

Some of our findings included:

  • Matt Ryan, the player with the highest AV in 2016 (also the league MVP), had a fair salary of $16.13 million in 2016.
  • The most overpaid player (using ‚Äòcap hit‚Äô as salary metric), in total dollars (not in percentage of salary cap) between 1994 and 2016 was Tony Romo in 2016, whose fair salary was 0, but whose cap hit was over 20 million
  • The most underpaid player (using ‚Äòcap hit‚Äô again as salary metric), in total dollars (not in percentage of salary cap) between 1994 and 2016 was Dak Prescott, whose fair salary was 12.3 million, and whose cap hit was 546,000.

These findings are interesting, but undervalue backups - i.e. some backups have 0 AV but are worth over $0 (i.e. backup quarterbacks).

Part Two: Draft Pick Historic Performance

Using a merged dataset containing annual AV data for each player (scraped from Pro Football Reference) and draft position for each player (also scraped from Pro Football Reference), we calculated the average AV per year by pick number. The data includes drafted players from 1994 to 2016 (1994 was the start of the salary cap era, though I suppose we could have gone back further, since we are looking purely at pick performance here).

We only looked at average AV per year over the first four years of a players‚Äô career, as that is the length of the rookie contract, the fifth year option for first round picks not withstanding.

We smooth our data by fitting it to a logarithmic regression. We get the following regression formula, with R^2 = 0.8676:

AV = 7.07347e^(-0.0099652 * [pick number])

Part Three: Calculating Fair Salary for Draft Picks

We now have a function representing the expected AV contributions for each pick number, and want to estimate a fair salary for each pick number as a result.

Our AV-to-salary converter (mentioned) in Part One required three parameters: SC (the total combined salary cap across all 32 teams), N (the total number of players who can be on an NFL roster), and AVTotal (the summed AV of the top N NFL players in a given season).

The important point here is that the fair salary of a draft pick depends on the salary cap that season! And we don‚Äôt have the salary cap numbers for the next four seasons. We make a few assumptions for estimating the fair salary of a 2017 NFL Draft pick:

  1. The salary cap stays the same over the next four years ‚Äì $167,000,000. So, SC = 167 million times 32.
  2. The NFL roster size stays at 53 and the number of teams stays at 32. So N = 53 times 32.
  3. The total AV in all of the next four years is approximated by the average total AV over the last five years (2012 - 2016), which comes out to 6559.

Assumptions #1 and #3 will almost certainly not be true. For assumption #1, knowing that the salary cap will likely increase, we can circle back later and note that our fair salary assumptions are probably conservative. Results and Discussion

In trading for Brock Osweiler this past Spring, the Browns essentially traded $15 million dollars for a second round draft pick. According to our model, an average second round draft pick provides just ~$8 million dollars in surplus, over all four years of the career.

Let‚Äôs go back to our bartering model. Imagine the assets of an NFL team (players, unused cap space, draft picks) as a big mountain of gold. In a fair transaction (i.e. free agency contract), an NFL team gives up a lump of gold for an equal-sized lump of gold, not increasing or decreasing their overall asset base.

With an average second round pick, an NFL has the opportunity to hand over a lump of gold worth $5.5 million (roughly the average salary over four years for second round pick) and receive in return a lump worth $13.5 million. Thus the asset base grows by $8 million. A good trade, for any barterer!

In order to make this transaction though, the Browns gave up $15 million in assets. This means the Browns in essence removed $15 million from their asset mountain, and only received $8 million in return.

So, does that mean the Browns made a bad trade? Not necessarily ‚Äì one explanation is that the Browns wanted to give up present value (they will eat the $15 million dollars this year only, when they don‚Äôt realistically have a shot at winning the Super Bowl) for future value (the surplus is spread over the next four years). In addition, there are intangible considerations - perhaps the Browns wanted more youth in the locker room.

Also keep in mind, as mentioned, that we assumed the salary cap stays constant in our analysis. If the cap increases as it has been (7-8% annually over the past three years), we can roughly estimate the surplus as being between $8 and $10 million ($10 million assumes that the cap is equal to 1.08^3 all four years, so it is an upper bound).

Other interesting findings:

  • Our results confirm the idea that draft picks tend to be underpaid.
  • High first round picks tend to be overpaid, and under the bartering model, the top six picks in the draft, if made, actually expect to provide negative surplus. In other words, our model is saying teams picking in the top six should actually pass on their pick (remember, each draft pick is actually an option)!
  • The most underpaid pick (with the highest surplus) is pick number 33, at the top of the second round. As a General Manager, this also means that trading down in the first round is generally a great strategy.
  • The draft is meant in some ways to instill parity in the NFL ‚Äì the better the team, the worse the pick. If the NFL really wants to institute parity, it should consider reducing the salaries of high first round picks further ‚Äì as of now, good teams have the advantage in the first round, as late first round picks grow the ‚Äògold mountain‚Äô more than early first round picks!

It‚Äôs easy to fall in the trap of equating high first round picks with superstars like Khalil Mack and Aaron Donald, who massively outperform their rookie contracts. But for every one of those players, there‚Äôs a Greg Robinson or Blake Bortles, who massively underperform their contracts. For General Managers who believe that they are only capable of selecting Khalil Mack‚Äôs and Aaron Donald‚Äôs, these results may mean nil ‚Äì but the data does seem to suggest on average, the high first round pick will not live up to their rookie contract.

These are impactful and meaningful insights, and provide promise for future work. However, we note the following limitations (which we could also address in future work):

  • The fifth year option for first round picks is not factored into the valuation. For first round picks, teams get a ‚Äòoption‚Äô to add an additional fifth year to the player‚Äôs contract, usually at a more expensive salary than the previous four years. For players selected in the first round of the 2014 NFL draft, 23 of the 32 fifth year options were picked up. Thus, we can expect first round picks to be worth slightly more than what our model suggests. Quantifying this would be a worthy next step.
  • As aforementioned, another flaw in our model is that we did not account for possible increases in salary cap. The salary cap has increased substantially over the past 23 years, and it is reasonable to expect it to continue to increase. Accounting for this would increase the ‚Äòfair salary‚Äô for the draft picks, and further increase the ‚Äòworth‚Äô of each draft pick. This means that our current estimates for pick worth are almost certainly conservative.
  • We estimated fair salary by assuming a linear relationship between AV and salary. We lacked a strong basis in making this assumption, and it‚Äôs worth testing to see if it‚Äôs real or not. A better option might just be to use market data ‚Äì see the part about Brian Burke‚Äôs project in the Important Notes section below.
  • Finally, it is important to note that Approximate Value is just as its name indicates, an ‚Äòapproximate‚Äô value of a player‚Äôs contribution. Creating a better metric could be a sizeable undertaking but would potentially improve results.

A final caveat to note is that these draft pick valuations are only valid for future, unknown draft picks. During the actual draft, these picks should be instead valued based the players available at each selection (which, of course, is not known until usually a few picks before).
